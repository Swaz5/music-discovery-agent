## Trip-Hop

### What It Sounds Like
Trip-hop sounds like the city at 3am — neon reflections in wet streets, something unresolved in the air. It takes hip-hop's breakbeat architecture and runs it through a filter of jazz, dub, soul, and existential dread. The tempos are slow and heavy, beats hitting with the weight of a headache rather than the snap of a dancefloor. Samples are layered with paranoid density: a snatch of vibraphone, a bass guitar that sounds like it came from underwater, a vocal fragment playing in reverse.

The production is crucial. Trip-hop producers treat the studio as an instrument, building sound from the friction between incongruous elements — a jazz loop against a hip-hop kick, a string section buried so deep in reverb it sounds like it's coming from another room. Vocals, when present, tend to be widescreen and melancholic: Portishead's Beth Gibbons sounds like she's performing at the end of the world.

Bristol, not London or New York, was ground zero — something about that city's particular mix of soundsystem culture, art school experimentalism, and post-industrial atmosphere produced exactly this music and no other.

### Key Artists
- **Massive Attack** — the originators. *Blue Lines* invented the genre in 1991; *Mezzanine* darkened it almost past the point of endurance a decade later.
- **Portishead** — took the template and made it more cinematic, more wounded, more extreme. *Dummy* is the most accessible; *Portishead* (the third album) is the most uncompromising.
- **Tricky** — the most paranoid voice in the genre, his records feeling genuinely disturbed in ways that still unsettle decades later.
- **DJ Shadow** — the American answer, treating crates of samples as raw material for dense, narrative instrumental albums. *Endtroducing...* is astonishing.
- **Björk** — not strictly trip-hop but *Post* and *Homogenic* share its sonic DNA and emotional register.
- **Unkle** — the cinematic, grandiose wing; *Psyence Fiction* is a flawed classic.
- **Bonobo** — carries the torch into the present with more warmth and less darkness.

### Subgenres & Related Styles
Trip-hop bleeds in several directions. Its jazz-sampling side connects it to **boom bap** hip-hop (Gang Starr, Pete Rock). Its electronic texture overlaps with **IDM** and **downtempo** (Boards of Canada, Kruder & Dorfmeister). The cinematic quality links it to **post-rock** (Mogwai) and **dark ambient**. Contemporary artists like **James Blake** and **Mount Kimbie** carry its spirit without copying its aesthetics. If you love trip-hop, nu-jazz (Jazzanova, Cinematic Orchestra) will feel like an adjacent room.

### The Vibe
Insomnia music. Headphones-only music. It rewards attention but tolerates distraction — it's just as effective as a backdrop to late-night work as it is for dedicated listening. Trip-hop excels at articulating complex, unresolvable feelings: grief that has calcified, desire that has gone cold, beauty that feels dangerous. Activities: writing, driving alone at night, the morning after something went wrong.
