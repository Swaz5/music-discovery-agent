## Psychedelic Rock

### What It Sounds Like
Psychedelic rock is guitar music that learned to distrust linear time. The song structures stretch and bend: a verse might repeat until it hypnotises, a solo might run for eight minutes without losing purpose, the whole arrangement might dissolve into a drone and reconstitute itself somewhere unexpected. Guitar tones are processed beyond recognition — backwards recordings, extreme reverb, fuzz that sounds like a saw, wah pedals used not as accent but as primary voice. Studio technology is weaponised: stereo panning that physically moves sounds across the room, orchestral overdubs, found sounds.

The tempo question is interesting. Classic psychedelia often settled into a mid-tempo groove — not slow enough to be meditative, not fast enough to be urgent, exactly the pace of something evolving. Modern psychedelia is more varied: Kevin Parker (Tame Impala) brings disco grooves to the aesthetic; King Gizzard plays psychedelic music at thrash tempo.

The genre is extraordinarily wide. Between the baroque orchestral arrangements of the Beatles' *Revolver*, the free-form improvisation of early Pink Floyd, the hard rock assault of early Hawkwind, and the intimate folk-influenced sound of Nick Drake (adjacent, at least), there is more distance than you might expect from a single genre label.

### Key Artists
- **The Beatles** — *Revolver*, *Sgt. Pepper's*, and *The White Album* cover so much ground within psychedelia that they function as a genre map unto themselves.
- **Pink Floyd** — two distinct phases: Syd Barrett's kaleidoscopic originals and the post-Barrett band's monumental cosmic rock. Both essential, neither like the other.
- **The Doors** — organ-driven and darkly erotic; Jim Morrison's baritone and Ray Manzarek's keyboard create a sound nobody has successfully replicated.
- **Tame Impala** — the undisputed contemporary heir; Kevin Parker's one-man studio project proves the aesthetic is not exhausted.
- **MGMT** — psychedelic pop for people who want hooks with their hallucinations; *Oracular Spectacular* is enormously fun.
- **King Gizzard and the Lizard Wizard** — prolific, genre-fluid, and genuinely exciting; *Nonagon Infinity* is a perfect loop and an excellent place to start.
- **Jefferson Airplane** — the San Francisco scene's finest; *Surrealistic Pillow* contains Grace Slick's two most perfect performances.

### Subgenres & Related Styles
**Neo-psychedelia** (MGMT, Tame Impala, Animal Collective) updates the aesthetic with modern production. **Krautrock** (Can, Neu!, Faust) is psychedelia filtered through German minimalism and motorik rhythms — connect them through the shared emphasis on hypnotic repetition. **Shoegaze** inherits the wall-of-sound guitar approach. **Space rock** (Hawkwind, Spiritualized) extends the cosmic dimension into harder territory. **Acid folk** (Vashti Bunyan, Incredible String Band) is psychedelia without the rock. For modern listeners arriving from indie, Tame Impala's *Currents* is a perfect on-ramp; for those coming from electronic music, Can's *Tago Mago* shows how far the genre could drift toward abstraction.

### The Vibe
Psychedelic rock is for altered states, real or metaphorical — the best of it changes how you hear the sounds around it, makes the room feel slightly different. It's for long drives where the landscape is doing something interesting, for late evenings with friends where conversation has become ideas rather than information, for the specific pleasure of a piece of music that goes somewhere unexpected and takes you with it. At its best, it is genuinely surprising even on the fifteenth listen.
