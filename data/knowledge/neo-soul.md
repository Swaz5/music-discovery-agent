## Neo-Soul

### What It Sounds Like
Neo-soul is what classic soul music sounds like when it goes to art school and comes back with more complicated feelings. It takes the warm, organic sound of 1970s soul and R&B — live drums, electric bass, Rhodes piano, horn sections — and runs it through a late-1990s sensibility that is less interested in romance and more interested in complexity. The production is often deliberately imperfect: slightly off-grid drums, tape hiss, records audibly worn at the edges.

The vocals are expressive in a way that leans into difficulty rather than easy melisma — singers like Erykah Badu, D'Angelo, and Lauryn Hill use restraint and timing as much as range and power. Harmonies are lush but unusual, borrowing from jazz. Lyrics are concerned with spiritual and political dimensions of love rather than just its pleasures. The overall effect is music that feels soulful in the original sense — rooted in something authentic — while refusing to be simple.

Tempos vary widely: neo-soul includes slow jams that feel almost motionless (*Brown Sugar*) and mid-tempo grooves that make you want to move without quite being able to categorise what you're doing (*On & On*).

### Key Artists
- **D'Angelo** — the centre of gravity. *Voodoo* (2000) is a jazz-funk masterpiece disguised as an R&B album; *Black Messiah* arrived 14 years later and was worth every year of the wait.
- **Erykah Badu** — *Baduizm* defined the genre's spiritual and aesthetic dimensions; her voice is one of the most distinctive instruments in popular music.
- **Lauryn Hill** — *The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill* fused neo-soul with hip-hop with a directness and emotional force that has never been equalled.
- **Maxwell** — smooth, romantic, and underrated; *Urban Hang Suite* is perfect late-night listening.
- **Common** — the hip-hop side of neo-soul, with J Dilla production making the connection between the two explicit.
- **Thundercat** — the contemporary heir, taking the jazz-bass dimension of neo-soul to virtuosic extremes.
- **H.E.R.** — carries the classic sound into the present with remarkable authenticity.
- **Steve Lacy** — the weirder, younger relative, adding indie guitar textures to the same organic palette.

### Subgenres & Related Styles
Neo-soul connects to **classic soul** (Marvin Gaye, Al Green) as its ancestor, **jazz fusion** (Herbie Hancock, Weather Report) as its harmonic model, and **alternative R&B** (Frank Ocean, The Weeknd) as its contemporary evolution. The **Soulquarians collective** (D'Angelo, Erykah Badu, Common, Questlove, J Dilla) was the scene's beating heart — everything connected to that group is worth your attention. If you love neo-soul, J Dilla's production work is the connective tissue between it and hip-hop.

### The Vibe
Neo-soul is intimate music for intimate situations. It's the genre for cooking dinner for someone you're falling in love with, for Saturday mornings with coffee and no agenda, for the warm melancholy of a relationship that is good but complicated. It resists the background — it always feels like it's about something — but it's comfortable enough to live with. The best neo-soul records feel like they belong to your personal history even on first listen.
