## Jazz Fusion

### What It Sounds Like
Jazz fusion is what happened when jazz musicians in the late 1960s started listening to Hendrix, Sly Stone, and James Brown and decided they wanted that electricity without abandoning improvisation. Electric instruments replaced acoustic ones: the upright bass gave way to the electric bass, the piano to Fender Rhodes and clavinet, and Miles Davis plugged in everything and pointed it somewhere new.

The sound is dense and rhythmically complex — fusion kept jazz's harmonic sophistication and improvisational core but married it to the groove, volume, and energy of rock and funk. Odd time signatures are common (7/4, 11/8) but used in service of something that still feels physical rather than merely clever. Guitar plays a much larger role than in traditional jazz: think wah-pedal leads over descending modal vamps. The production moved away from the acoustic warmth of Blue Note toward something harder and more electric.

At its best, fusion is genuinely thrilling — music at the technical ceiling of what small groups of humans can play together. At its worst, it became expensive-sounding background music for airport lounges, which is why the word "fusion" acquired unfortunate connotations. The good stuff is nowhere near that.

### Key Artists
- **Miles Davis** — the necessary starting point; *Bitches Brew* (1970) is the genre's founding document, a double album of controlled chaos that still sounds futuristic.
- **Mahavishnu Orchestra** — John McLaughlin's guitar at maximum intensity; *The Inner Mounting Flame* is relentless and technically staggering.
- **Herbie Hancock** — bridged jazz and funk on *Head Hunters* (1973) in a way that made both richer.
- **Weather Report** — more textural and atmospheric than other fusion bands; *Heavy Weather* contains "Birdland," one of the most joyful pieces of music ever recorded.
- **Chick Corea** — prolific and melodically generous; *Return to Forever* ranges from delicate to explosive.
- **Pat Metheny** — the most approachable entry point for rock listeners; his sound is warm, melodic, and deeply musical.
- **Kamasi Washington** — the contemporary inheritor, bringing fusion's ambition into conversation with hip-hop and neo-soul.

### Subgenres & Related Styles
**Smooth jazz** is fusion's commercial lobotomy — the textures without the risk. Avoid. **Neo-soul** shares fusion's love of live instrumentation and harmonic sophistication; D'Angelo's *Voodoo* is effectively a jazz-funk record. **Afrobeat** (Fela Kuti) is a parallel development that arrived at similar territory from different coordinates. If you're approaching from hip-hop, J Dilla's production borrows heavily from fusion breaks. Contemporary jazz (Thundercat, Robert Glasper Experiment) exists in direct conversation with the genre.

### The Vibe
Jazz fusion rewards attention but doesn't require it. It's ideal for work that needs sustained focus without lyrics competing for language-processing bandwidth. It also works exceptionally well for cooking elaborate meals — there's something about its structured complexity that pairs with the same quality in ambitious recipes. For dedicated listening, it's best experienced loud, at a volume where the bass is felt rather than just heard.
